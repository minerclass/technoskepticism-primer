Technoskepticism

Neither rejection nor surrender. A disciplined way of asking whether technological change stays answerable to human and educational purposes.

Technoskepticism is not opposition to technology. It is an insistence that technological change remain answerable to human purposes.

Compiled by Micah J. Miner · micahminer.com · A companion to the AAACS 2026 presentation Pedagogical Friction as Subversive Praxis

What it is

A stance, not a verdict

Technoskepticism is the practice of questioning technologies rather than accepting or refusing them on faith. It treats every tool — including generative AI — as non-neutral: a thing that carries values, redistributes power, and reorganizes the settings it enters. The point is not to slow innovation but to keep it accountable to the purposes of education.

Technoskepticism is

Disciplined questioning. Naming trade-offs, winners and losers, embedded assumptions, ripple effects, and the myths that make a tool feel inevitable.

Technoskepticism is not

Technophobia or refusal. It does not assume technologies are bad — it assumes they are consequential, and that those consequences deserve deliberation.

The framework

The five technoskeptical questions

Adapted by Dan Krutka and Scott Metzger from Neil Postman’s 1998 address, and circulated through the Civics of Technology curriculum. Select a question for its Postman root, a generative-AI classroom prompt, and a pedagogical-friction overlay.

Roots

Two frameworks, one lineage

The technoskeptical questions are an adaptation, not a replacement. They translate Postman’s five propositions about technological change into questions a class can actually ask. Questions 1, 2, 4, and 5 track Postman closely; question 3 reframes his claim that every technology carries an embedded idea into what the technology demands of us.

Postman (1998) — five propositionsCivics of Technology — five technoskeptical questions
1. Every technology is a trade-off (a price is always paid).What does society give up for the benefits of the technology?
2. There are always winners and losers.Who is harmed and who benefits from the technology?
3. Every technology has an embedded idea or philosophy.What does the technology need?
4. Technological change is ecological, not additive.What are the unintended or unexpected changes caused by the technology?
5. Media tend to become mythic — perceived as part of the natural order.Why is it difficult to imagine our world without the technology?
The overlay

From skeptical questions to a curricular stance

Technoskepticism asks whether a technology serves our purposes. Pedagogical friction asks the next question: which difficulties are doing the educational work, so that we protect them instead of automating them away. The five questions surface the trade-offs; the friction lens decides which forms of struggle are worth keeping.

Productive friction

Difficulty that builds capacity — cognitive labor, rhetorical struggle, ownership of one’s thinking, and institutional deliberation. The kind that must not disappear.

Exclusionary friction

Difficulty that restricts access — barriers that shut learners out rather than develop them. The kind that should be removed or redesigned.

Pedagogical friction keeps the curriculum conversation from being auto-completed.

Applied stance

Putting the questions to work

Technoskepticism earns its keep when it moves from abstract caution to disciplined review. For a practitioner-scholar leading technology across schools, the five questions sharpen four everyday decisions.

AI governance

Ask how a system handles data, privacy, bias, procurement, vendor power, staff workload, and long-term institutional dependence before treating it as a classroom solution.

Curriculum

Ask whether the tool preserves the interpretive, rhetorical, and reflective work through which students build durable understanding.

Equity

Ask which difficulties build capacity and which restrict access, participation, or demonstration without educational benefit.

Public responsibility

Ask when convenience, efficiency, or polish begins to displace human judgment, professional deliberation, and democratic accountability.
